1 Phase One

Triage — Assess the Damage

Understand the full scope of the crisis before taking any action. Resist the urge to fix — first, see clearly.

Review Account History

Audit the account timeline: contracts, past touchpoints, escalation history, previous interventions. Understand what was tried and why it failed.

Assess Four Dimensions

Trust: How does the customer feel about the partnership? Adoption: How deeply are they using the product? Issues: What open complaints exist? Stakeholders: Who are the key players and what are their stances?

Classify the Root Cause

Is this a product problem, a process problem, or a people problem? Most churn risks are not product failures — they are breakdowns in enablement, communication, or internal processes at the customer.

Identify Your Point of Contact

Find the person on the customer side who will be your partner in recovery. This person must be invested in success and willing to collaborate closely.

Key Insight

Don't diagnose until you've listened. Don't prescribe until you've diagnosed. A clear-eyed assessment with no sugarcoating is the foundation everything else is built on.

Phase Output

A clear-eyed assessment of account health, a root cause hypothesis, and an identified POC ready to partner on recovery.

2 Phase Two

Discover — Listen Deep, Diagnose Root Causes

Build trust through genuine listening while surfacing the real problems behind the stated complaints.

Start With the POC

Meet your Point of Contact first. Let them tell the full story — their frustrations, their teams' pain, what they've tried. Listen without defending. Your job here is to absorb, not to respond.

Interview Every Team

Conduct individual interviews with every impacted team. Ask: What are your real complaints? What would you want to achieve with the platform? What business questions are you trying to answer?

Perform a Technical Audit

Separate platform issues from usage issues. Audit the customer's implementation, data taxonomy, and configuration. Often, the problem is not the tool — it's how the tool was set up and is being used.

Map Stated vs. Actual Problems

Many complaints are symptoms, not causes. A user saying "this feature doesn't work" may actually be revealing a knowledge gap. Surface these gaps with empathy, then demonstrate what's possible.

Identify Silos

Look for teams working in isolation who share similar goals. Siloed processes are one of the biggest barriers to value realisation — and one of the biggest opportunities for you to create impact by connecting people.

Reframe Business Questions

Assess whether the customer's questions are reporting-oriented ("How many subscribers in France?") or decision-oriented ("What behaviours predict churn in France, and what's our target?"). The former produces dashboards. The latter produces outcomes.

Key Technique

Challenge with empathy. When a complaint stems from a knowledge gap, don't say "you're wrong" — demonstrate the capability. Let the revelation come from their own experience. When business questions are report-oriented, gently reframe toward decisions and targets.

Phase Output

Root cause analysis, stakeholder map with individual needs, knowledge gap inventory, and a clear picture of what needs to change — at the platform level and the process level.

3 Phase Three

Plan — Chart the Recovery

Define a structured, transparent recovery roadmap. The customer must believe in this plan because they helped shape it.

Create the Action Plan

Build a clear plan with milestones, owners, and timelines. Share it openly with the customer. Transparency is non-negotiable — it's the first step in rebuilding trust.

Heal Before You Build

No upsells. No new feature pitches. No expansion conversations. The infected wound must be cleaned first. Resolve existing pain before proposing anything new. Sales motions at this stage will destroy what little trust remains.

Align Internal Resources

Coordinate with Value Engineering, Training, Product, and Executive Sponsors. Define who does what. If internal ownership is lacking, be prepared to carry the weight yourself.

Establish a Cadence

Set up regular check-ins with your POC. Consistent presence signals commitment. Unreliable follow-up signals the customer is not a priority — even if that's not the intent.

Key Insight

The plan exists to rebuild trust as much as to organise work. A customer who can see the roadmap, who helped define it, and who sees you executing on it — that customer starts to believe the partnership can work.

Phase Output

A recovery action plan with milestones, clear ownership, an established cadence, and internal alignment on who delivers what.

4 Phase Four

Deliver — Create Undeniable Value

Prove the platform's value through tangible, tailored outcomes. This is where the rescue is won or lost — in the work itself.

Build Tailored Solutions

Create custom dashboards and analyses addressing each team's specific business questions. Don't just advise — build. Show, don't tell. The deliverables must speak for themselves.

Coach the POC

Invest heavily in 1:1 technical coaching with your Point of Contact. Build their internal expertise so they become the internal champion who can sustain momentum after you step back.

Run Cross-Team Workshops

Bring siloed teams together when you discover they share objectives. Joint workshops create alignment, reduce duplication, and demonstrate platform value across the organisation — not just within one team.

Be Proactively Generous

Anticipate needs and deliver before being asked. Think about what problems or business questions people might have and build solutions proactively. Go further than expected — open your own account with the product, create demo environments, build proof-of-concept workflows.

Use Competitive Benchmarking

"Your competitors use the same platform and are successful. The gap isn't the product — it's in process." This reframes the conversation from blame to empowerment. The customer has agency to close the gap.

Deliver On-Site When Possible

Physical presence compounds trust. If you can run workshops on-site, do it. The investment of time and effort signals commitment at a level that remote engagement cannot match.

Core Principle

Create strategic dependency through value. By doing the work — building dashboards, running analyses, coaching individuals — you become indispensable. The customer's experience shifts from scepticism to reliance. This is the turning point of every rescue.

Phase Output

Tangible deliverables per team, an upskilled POC, cross-team alignment, and a customer who has experienced the platform's value firsthand — not been told about it.

5 Phase Five

Elevate — Executive Alignment & ROI

Lock in the value story at the executive level with quantified business impact. Individual team wins must roll up into an organisational narrative.

Executive Business Review

Organise an EBR with senior leadership from both sides. This is not a product demo — it's a strategic alignment session. Bring your executives to the table; it signals that this customer matters at the highest level.

Build ROI Frameworks

Connect platform usage to business outcomes with concrete numbers. Develop measurement frameworks — e.g., quantifying the revenue impact of technical errors, or correlating adoption metrics to business KPIs.

Let the Customer Speak

By this point, the customer should be voicing the transformation themselves. Their words carry more weight than yours. Create the conditions for this — but don't script it. Authentic advocacy is earned, not manufactured.

Align on the Future

The EBR is not just a look-back — it's a look-forward. What does the next chapter of the partnership look like? What use cases can be expanded? What's the shared vision? This sets the stage for renewal.

Key Insight

Reusable assets created during this phase — such as ROI measurement frameworks — have value beyond a single account. Document them. Share them across the organisation. The best playbooks generate compounding returns.

Phase Output

Executive-level validation of the partnership's value, a quantified ROI story, shared forward-looking strategy, and reusable frameworks for the broader CS/PS organisation.

6 Phase Six

Bridge — From Recovery to Renewal

Transition the rescued account from recovery mode into a healthy, sustainable renewal conversation.

Validate Sentiment Shift

Confirm the transformation is real — not just with your POC, but across multiple stakeholders. A rescue isn't complete until the organisation's perception has shifted, not just one person's.

Create Success Plans

Document comprehensive success plans for each team and use case. These serve as the handoff document — they ensure continuity regardless of who owns the account going forward.

Warm Handoff to Sales

Arm Sales with the full context: what was broken, what was fixed, the ROI story, and the customer's current sentiment. The customer should enter renewal conversations from a position of confidence, not resentment.

Don't Disappear

Maintain your personal connection with the POC and key stakeholders through the renewal period. A sudden absence after months of intense engagement will feel like abandonment — and can undo trust.

Key Insight

The rescue is not complete at renewal. It's complete when the account is self-sustaining — when the customer has internal champions, working processes, and the confidence to extract value independently. Your goal is to make yourself unnecessary, even as you made yourself indispensable.

Phase Output

A customer entering renewal from a position of trust. Documented success plans. A Sales team armed with context and confidence. And a relationship that can sustain beyond the rescue.

The Foundation

10 Core Principles

These principles aren't theoretical. They were forged in the field — through real rescues, real pressure, and real outcomes.

01

Extreme Ownership

When no one else steps up, you step up. Own the outcome entirely. The customer doesn't care about internal ownership gaps — they care about results.

02

Listen Before You Act

Deep listening builds trust and reveals root causes. The first meeting is for absorbing — not defending, not solutioning, not pitching.

03

Heal Before You Build

Address existing pain before proposing anything new. Clean the wound first. Sales motions on a frustrated customer accelerate churn — they don't prevent it.

04

Do the Work

Don't just advise. Build the dashboards. Run the analysis. Create the demos. Tangible deliverables build more trust than any number of strategy decks.

05

Challenge With Respect

Validate the customer's feelings, but challenge their assumptions when evidence supports it. Knowledge gaps masquerading as product complaints are your biggest opportunity.

06

Break the Silos

Look for teams with shared goals who don't know they share them. Bringing people together across organisational boundaries multiplies the value of every engagement.

07

Reframe the Questions

Shift customers from "what are the numbers?" to "what decisions will you make?" Report-oriented questions produce dashboards. Decision-oriented questions produce outcomes.

08

Be Proactively Generous

Anticipate needs and deliver before being asked. The best way to rebuild trust is to consistently exceed expectations — especially when no one is watching.

09

Create Strategic Dependency

Make yourself indispensable through consistent, high-quality delivery. The customer's trust shifts from scepticism to reliance — and that reliance carries into the renewal conversation.

10

Use Competitive Pressure Wisely

Benchmark against peers to motivate, not to intimidate. "The platform is the same for everyone — the difference is process." This empowers customers to close their own gaps.

In Practice

Case Study: Canal+

A global media company on the verge of churn — rescued through disciplined execution of this playbook.

Before

  • Zero trust in the platform across the organisation
  • Product perceived as "only good for reading numbers, not taking action"
  • Minimal adoption — limited to basic reporting
  • Director of Data planning to remove key use cases from taxonomy
  • Multiple teams with open complaints and unresolved issues
  • Relationship health at its lowest point
  • Siloed teams with no cross-functional collaboration

After

  • Complete sentiment transformation across all stakeholder levels
  • Platform recognised as a strategic decision-making tool
  • Increased adoption with tailored dashboards per team
  • Customer publicly praised the turnaround of the partnership
  • Teams connected around shared objectives and workflows
  • Account moved from active churn risk to renewal negotiations
  • ROI framework adopted as a reusable asset for other accounts

What Was Done

  1. Conducted a comprehensive data and taxonomy audit — confirmed the problem was implementation and process, not platform capability.
  2. Interviewed every impacted team individually. Surfaced real needs, knowledge gaps, and siloed processes that had been invisible from the outside.
  3. Reframed business questions from reporting-oriented to decision-oriented. Helped teams define target metrics and actionable KPIs instead of passive dashboards.
  4. Built tailored dashboards and analyses for each team's specific business questions — hands-on, not advisory.
  5. Delivered intensive 1:1 technical coaching with the customer's POC, building internal expertise and a genuine champion.
  6. Created proof-of-concept demonstrations using real product data — including opening a personal account to generate authentic demo content.
  7. Brought siloed teams together once shared objectives were identified, creating cross-functional alignment for the first time.
  8. Delivered 17 on-site workshops in Paris — a concentrated investment that compounded trust and demonstrated commitment.
  9. Developed a reusable framework for measuring the revenue impact of technical errors — later adopted by other accounts.
  10. Coordinated an Executive Business Review with C-suite leadership, presenting an ROI-based strategy for the partnership's future.

What Not to Do

Common Anti-Patterns

Patterns that undermine rescues. Recognise them early — in yourself, in your team, or in your organisation's processes.

Pitching During Pain

Responding to customer frustrations with sales pitches or upsell proposals. The customer is describing an open wound — they don't want to hear about new features.

Instead: Acknowledge the pain. Fix what's broken. Only discuss expansion once trust is restored.

Surface-Level Check-Ins

Running status calls that ask "how's everything going?" without substance. At-risk customers need evidence of progress, not pleasantries.

Instead: Show up with deliverables. Every interaction should demonstrate tangible progress.

Ownership Diffusion

Multiple people "involved" but no one accountable. The customer experiences confusion, contradictory messages, and nothing getting done.

Instead: One person owns the rescue. Others support. The customer has one throat to choke and one hand to shake.

Advising Without Doing

Telling the customer what they should do without building it yourself. Strategy without execution is just a slide deck gathering dust.

Instead: Build the dashboard. Create the analysis. Do the work — then walk them through it.

Ignoring Internal Silos

Treating each team as an isolated engagement. You miss the compounding value of connecting teams with shared objectives.

Instead: Map shared goals across teams. Create joint workshops. Let teams discover their common ground.

Vanishing After the Win

Disengaging once sentiment improves but before the renewal closes. The customer feels used — trust evaporates right when it matters most.

Instead: Stay present through renewal. The bridge phase is as important as the rescue itself.
